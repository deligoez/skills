# Foundations — why typography matters and how to judge it

The reasoning behind the rules. Read this when you need to *justify* a recommendation or make a
judgment call the checklists don't cover. Every specific rule in the other files descends from
these principles.

## What typography is

**"Typography is the visual component of the written word."** Text is the content — it stays the
same whether spoken, printed, or shown on screen. Typography is how that text *looks* when
displayed. Font choice is only one part of it; size, spacing, and layout are equally typography.

Typography is **utilitarian, not primarily an art** (the analogy is photography — it can rise to
art, but its core job is functional). Effectiveness comes from *skill*, not innate taste, and the
skill is learnable by anyone who can form opinions. This matters for how you advise: you are not
chasing prettiness, you are making the text do its job better.

## Who it is for

**"Typography is for the benefit of the reader, not the writer."** Legal writers can't hand
design off to a professional the way a novelist can, so they must serve the reader themselves —
and the real reader is nothing like the imagined one:

| | Writer | Real reader | Imagined reader |
|---|---|---|---|
| Attention span | long | short | whatever it takes |
| Interest in topic | high | low | boundless |
| Open to other views | no | yes | barely |
| Cares about writer | yes | no | of course |

"The only reader who might match [the imagined] description is your mother." Design for the reader
who is busy, skeptical, and looking for a reason to stop reading.

## Why it matters

Good typography conserves the reader's **attention** — "the most valuable resource you have as a
writer." Two laws:

- **First Law:** readers lean on typography to judge a document exactly when they can't easily
  judge it on substance.
- **Second Law:** judging substance takes attention, so readers with little attention fall back on
  judging by typography.

Readers are, in effect, looking for an excuse to quit; once their attention is gone, persuasion is
impossible. Good typography lets them "devote less attention to the mechanics of reading and more
attention to your message." It is **not** more important than good writing — but it makes good
writing better, and bad typography undercuts it. (Setting an entire brief in one visual monotone —
all 12-pt Times New Roman — is like delivering oral argument in a flat monotone. And readers form
a judgment on a résumé within about two seconds, from presentation alone.)

## What "good" means

**Good typography makes the text more effective** — it reinforces the text's goals. Three
corollaries:

1. **Context decides.** Quality is measured against *this* text's purpose, not an abstract
   standard. "One size never fits all."
2. **Many valid answers.** There's rarely a single correct solution; several can serve equally.
3. **Understanding over aesthetics.** You must understand what the text is trying to do. Miss that,
   and good typography becomes "purely a matter of luck."

So the self-test for any document is: **"What do readers want from this document, and is the
typography helping them get it?"** A design that looks nice but buries the relief being sought is a
failure; an unglamorous one that surfaces it is a success. (This is why all-caps contract clauses
backfire — they *look* emphatic but read slower, so readers skip exactly what you wanted noticed.)

## Where the rules come from

**"There is no 'legal typography.' There is only typography."** Legal documents are held to the
same standard as any professional publishing — there's no separate lesser standard. Lawyers are,
functionally, **publishers**: responsible for every step from composing a document to delivering
it to a reader. Bad habits come from two places: typing-class instruction (typewriter-era
conventions) and "boilerplate syndrome" — reluctance to deviate from templates that "worked"
before, some dating back decades.

The old excuse (typewriters couldn't match professional typesetting) is gone: modern word
processors and laser printers can. Butterick's stance, which is a good default for judgment calls:
**"When faced with a choice between more consistency with professional typography or less, choose
more consistency"** — while taking "a few shortcuts where the effort outweighs the results," because
legal practice has real time constraints.

Reject the "crystal goblet" idea (Beatrice Warde's 1932 essay that typography should be
*invisible*). Text has no visible form without typography, so "invisible typography" is incoherent.
**"The written word is a fusion of text and typography, substance and presentation."** Typography
is like seasoning in a dish — it doesn't change the nutrition, but it changes how much gets eaten.

## Two technical concepts worth knowing

- **The em.** The em is a unit equal to a font's point size — the total vertical space budget for
  the type, *not* the width of the letter "M" and *not* the cap height. This is why two fonts at the
  same point size can look different in size (each uses its em differently), and why you match sizes
  by adjusting until line breaks align rather than trusting the number. Point size has *never* meant
  cap height — if capitals filled the whole em, descenders (g, p, y) would have nowhere to go. This
  isn't academic: a 2012 Michigan Supreme Court case (election law requiring "14-point boldfaced
  type") turned on it — the court correctly held point size means the em size, not the height of the
  capitals, in an "alarmingly close" vote.
- **Screen vs. print has mostly converged.** For ~20 years screens were stuck near 75 dpi, which is
  why screen-optimized fonts (Verdana, Georgia, Calibri) existed. Modern displays (185–326 dpi)
  exceed the eye's resolution limit (~1–2 arcminutes; ~143 dpi at 24″, ~286 dpi at 12″), so those
  fonts have no special screen benefit today. **Use the font you'd choose for print.** Only shrink
  margins / enlarge type for screen if a document will be read *exclusively* on screen; anything
  that might be printed (especially court filings) keeps the print-optimized layout.

## Is there a "best font"?

No single objectively best font, but it's "not a matter of whimsy" either. Legibility research is
real (Bell Centennial's ink traps for tiny phone-book type; Clearview for highway signs) but
muddied because familiarity biases what readers *perceive* as legible. Method: reason about the
document type (a contract is not a slide deck), let context narrow the field, then choose within it
— the final pick still involves "art, humanity, and expressiveness."

## How far to push it

Two cautionary principles for real practice:

- **Judges get the last word on formatting.** In the 2014 Deepwater Horizon litigation, Judge
  Barbier rebuked BP for cramming 27 lines onto pages that "should have only 23" (slightly-under-
  double spacing to fit more content). You may gently educate a judge in low-stakes situations, but
  never try to "correct" a judge's reading of the rules in high-stakes litigation. Comply in good
  faith and you're on solid ground.
- **Typography is a vessel for care.** Butterick's afterword: lawyers rise or fall on the *care*
  they put into a case, and the written word is the primary vessel for that care. Typography is "one
  more way of expressing the care that's essential to our work. What you get out of it depends on
  what you put in."
